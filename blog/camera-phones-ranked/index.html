Camera Phone Rankings 2025: Who Takes the Best Photos?

Tech Editor September 5, 2025 15 min read
SamsungAppleXiaomiCamera Rankings

We shot the same scenes, at the same times of day, across every camera phone in this ranking, from full flagships to budget models under $300. This guide ranks the best camera phones of 2025 by what actually matters: zoom range, low-light detail, video quality, and how consistent the results are without extra editing.

Camera marketing has gotten harder to trust every year. A phone with a bigger megapixel number on the box does not automatically take better photos than one with a smaller number, and a phone with four rear lenses is not automatically better than one with two. What actually separates a great camera phone from an average one is sensor size, lens quality, image processing software, and how consistent the results look without you needing to tap around in a pro mode to fix them.

This ranking cuts through the spec-sheet noise. We tested each phone's main camera, ultra-wide lens, zoom performance, low-light shots, video recording, and selfie camera side by side, then ranked them by real photo and video quality rather than raw numbers. Whether you have $250 or $1,300 to spend, you will find a clear recommendation below.

How We Ranked These Camera Phones

Every phone was tested against the same five criteria:

  • Main camera detail and color accuracy — sharpness and true-to-life color in daylight and indoor lighting.
  • Zoom quality — how much detail survives at 3x, 5x, and beyond, comparing true optical zoom against digital cropping.
  • Low-light performance — noise levels and detail retention in dim indoor and nighttime shots.
  • Video recording — stabilization, color consistency, and dynamic range across resolutions and frame rates.
  • Selfie camera quality — detail and skin tone accuracy from the front-facing camera.

A $270 phone was never expected to out-shoot a $1,300 flagship in every category. Instead, we asked how well each phone performs relative to its price, since that is the only fair way to compare a budget camera phone against a flagship one.

Best Overall Camera: Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra

The Galaxy S25 Ultra remains the phone to beat if zoom range is your top priority. Its 200MP main sensor delivers exceptional detail in good light, and its dual telephoto lenses give it the longest practical zoom range of any phone in this ranking, useful for travel photography, wildlife, and sports where getting physically closer to the subject is not an option.

Low-light performance is also excellent, with the large main sensor gathering enough light to keep noise low even in dim indoor settings. The trade-off is that Samsung's processing can occasionally push colors and contrast a little further than a strictly neutral, true-to-life shot, something some photographers prefer and others find slightly heavy-handed. If you want the single most versatile camera system available, this is it.

Best Video Camera: Apple iPhone 16 Pro Max

The iPhone 16 Pro Max is the strongest choice for anyone who records more video than photos. Its 48MP main sensor produces sharp, consistent color across a wide range of lighting conditions without heavy processing, and its video stabilization remains the smoothest of any phone we tested, particularly while walking or filming handheld.

Where it trails the Galaxy S25 Ultra is zoom range, since its telephoto lens tops out at a shorter optical distance. For most everyday shooting, including portraits, group photos, and casual video, that gap rarely matters. For anyone shooting distant subjects regularly, it is worth knowing before you buy.

Best Value Flagship Camera: Xiaomi 15 Pro

The Xiaomi 15 Pro uses a 50MP Leica-tuned main sensor that punches well above its price point, delivering color science that rivals phones costing several hundred dollars more. Daylight shots in particular show excellent contrast and natural skin tones, a direct result of Leica's involvement in tuning the camera's processing pipeline.

Low-light shots hold up reasonably well too, though noise becomes slightly more visible than on the Galaxy S25 Ultra once you move into very dim conditions. For buyers who want flagship-level photo quality without paying full flagship price, this is one of the strongest options available in 2025.

Best Camera Under $600: Realme GT 6

The Realme GT 6 punches above its weight thanks to a Sony IMX906 main sensor, a well-regarded sensor known for strong dynamic range and detail retention in daylight. Photos taken outdoors show impressive sharpness for the price, and the camera app's default processing produces natural-looking results without needing manual adjustment.

Its weaker point is low light, where detail softens noticeably compared to the flagship phones above it. If most of your photography happens outdoors or in well-lit spaces, the Realme GT 6 offers some of the best camera value under $600 in this entire ranking.

Best Zoom on a Budget: Tecno Phantom X2 Pro

The Tecno Phantom X2 Pro stands out for including a periscope telephoto lens, a feature normally reserved for phones costing well over $1,000. This gives it a genuine zoom advantage over other phones in its price bracket, letting you capture distant subjects with far less quality loss than a typical mid-range phone relying on digital zoom alone.

Its main camera is solid rather than exceptional, and low-light shots show more visible noise than the pricier phones on this list, but for buyers specifically prioritizing zoom range on a limited budget, it remains one of the more interesting camera phones available.

Best Budget Camera Overall: Infinix GT 20 Pro

The Infinix GT 20 Pro is the clearest budget recommendation on this list. Its 108MP main camera produces sharp, well-detailed daylight photos that hold up surprisingly well against phones costing twice as much, and its 144Hz AMOLED display makes reviewing and editing shots on the phone itself a genuinely pleasant experience.

Low light and zoom are the two areas where it falls furthest behind the flagships, unsurprising at its price point. For anyone shopping strictly under $300 who wants the sharpest possible everyday photos, this is the phone to consider.

Editor's Note Camera rankings can shift noticeably between a phone's default auto mode and its dedicated "Pro" or manual mode. Every ranking in this guide reflects default auto camera settings, since that is how most people actually shoot day to day.

Camera Specs Explained Simply

Does More Megapixels Mean Better Photos?

Megapixels measure resolution, or how much detail a photo can technically hold, not how good the photo actually looks. A well-tuned 50MP sensor with a larger physical size can outperform a poorly processed 200MP sensor in real-world shots. Once a phone passes roughly 48MP, additional megapixels matter far less than sensor size, lens quality, and software processing.

What Does Aperture (f-number) Mean?

Aperture, shown as an f-number like f/1.8 or f/2.2, describes how much light a lens lets in. A lower f-number lets in more light, which generally means better performance in dim conditions. Most main camera lenses on the phones in this ranking sit between f/1.6 and f/1.9, while ultra-wide and telephoto lenses typically use a narrower aperture.

Optical vs Digital Zoom

Optical zoom uses a physical lens to magnify an image without losing detail, while digital zoom simply crops into the existing image and enlarges it, which reduces sharpness. A phone advertising "100x zoom" is almost always combining a much shorter optical zoom with heavy digital cropping. Always check a phone's actual optical zoom number, not its maximum combined zoom figure, before assuming it will take sharp zoomed-in photos.

What Is Optical Image Stabilization (OIS)?

OIS physically shifts the camera lens or sensor to counteract small hand movements while you shoot, reducing blur in both photos and video. Nearly every phone in this ranking includes OIS on its main camera, though budget phones sometimes omit it from ultra-wide or telephoto lenses, which is worth checking if zoom or wide shots matter to you specifically.

What Is Sensor Size and Why Does It Matter More Than Megapixels?

Sensor size refers to the physical dimensions of the chip capturing light inside the camera module, often described using measurements like 1/1.3-inch or 1/1.56-inch. A larger sensor captures more light per pixel, which generally means better detail and less noise, especially in dim conditions. Two phones can share the same megapixel count while using very differently sized sensors, which is one of the main reasons camera quality varies so much between phones with seemingly similar spec sheets.

Low Light Photography: What Actually Helps

Low-light photo quality depends on more than a single spec. Sensor size determines how much light the camera can physically gather, aperture determines how much of that light reaches the sensor, and software processing, often called computational photography, determines how the phone combines multiple frames to reduce noise and brighten shadows without introducing blur. The Galaxy S25 Ultra and iPhone 16 Pro Max lead this ranking in low light specifically because they combine larger sensors with more advanced computational processing than the phones below them.

Video Recording: What to Look For

If video matters to you as much as photos, prioritize three things over any other spec: stabilization quality while walking or moving, consistent color and exposure between the main and ultra-wide lenses, and support for higher frame rates like 4K at 60fps if you plan to slow down footage in editing. The iPhone 16 Pro Max leads this ranking specifically because Apple's video processing keeps color and exposure closely matched across all of its lenses, something several competitors still handle less consistently.

Best Camera Phone by Use Case

Ranking phones by overall score is useful, but the right camera phone often depends on what you actually shoot most. Use the breakdown below to match a phone to your specific habits rather than a general ranking alone.

Best for Travel Photography

Travel photography demands versatility more than any single strength, since you might shoot a wide landscape, a distant landmark, and a low-light street scene all within the same afternoon. The Galaxy S25 Ultra's combination of a strong main sensor and long zoom range makes it the most well-rounded travel camera in this ranking, letting you capture both wide architecture shots and distant details without switching phones or lenses.

Best for Portraits and Everyday Social Photos

If most of your photography involves people, whether portraits, group shots, or casual social media content, the iPhone 16 Pro Max's natural skin tone rendering and consistent exposure make it a dependable choice that requires the least editing afterward. The Xiaomi 15 Pro is a close second here, with its Leica-tuned color science producing similarly flattering, natural-looking portraits at a lower price.

Best for Content Creators and Video

Anyone recording video regularly for social platforms should prioritize stabilization and consistent color across lenses over raw megapixel count. The iPhone 16 Pro Max remains the strongest pick here, though the Galaxy S25 Ultra is a capable alternative if you also want the flexibility of its longer zoom range for B-roll or detail shots.

Best for Night Photography

Low-light shooting rewards larger sensors and strong computational processing more than any other category. The Galaxy S25 Ultra and iPhone 16 Pro Max both handle night shots noticeably better than the rest of this ranking, while the Xiaomi 15 Pro offers a reasonable middle ground for buyers who shoot in dim conditions occasionally rather than as a primary use case.

How Computational Photography Actually Works

Modern phone cameras rarely capture a single frame and call it done. Instead, most phones use a process called computational photography, where the camera captures several frames in rapid succession, sometimes at different exposure levels, and then combines them into a single photo using software. This is how a phone camera with a physically tiny sensor, far smaller than what you would find in a dedicated camera, can still produce a sharp, well-exposed photo in a dim room.

This process is also why two phones with nearly identical hardware specifications can produce noticeably different photos. Samsung, Apple, and Xiaomi each use their own proprietary processing algorithms, which is why color, contrast, and sharpening can look meaningfully different between brands even when the underlying camera hardware is similar on paper.

Should You Buy a New Flagship Just for the Camera?

If photography is genuinely your top priority and you are considering upgrading from a phone that is two or more years old, a new flagship camera system will likely feel like a significant improvement, particularly in zoom range and low-light detail. If your current phone is a flagship from the past one to two years, the year-over-year camera improvements are usually smaller than marketing suggests, and the money may be better spent on a lens attachment, a tripod, or additional storage instead.

It is also worth considering that some of the biggest photography improvements each year come through software updates rather than new hardware. Both Apple and Samsung have shipped meaningful camera processing improvements to existing phones through free software updates, so checking whether your current phone has the latest camera software installed is a sensible first step before assuming you need to upgrade.

Common Camera Buying Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Choosing a phone based on megapixels alone. A high megapixel count with a small sensor often performs worse in real photos than a lower megapixel count with a larger sensor.
  2. Trusting advertised "zoom" numbers without checking the optical zoom figure. Combined optical-plus-digital zoom numbers can be misleading compared to the phone's true optical range.
  3. Ignoring the ultra-wide and telephoto lenses. Some budget phones include a strong main camera paired with a much weaker secondary lens, which only becomes obvious once you actually use it.
  4. Overlooking video quality if you record often. A phone with an excellent main camera for photos is not automatically the best choice if smooth, well-stabilized video matters more to you.
  5. Judging low-light performance from marketing photos alone. Always look for independent sample shots taken in genuinely dim conditions rather than brightly lit "night mode" demo images.

People Also Ask

Which phone has the best camera in 2025?
The Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra and Apple iPhone 16 Pro Max are the strongest overall performers in 2025, with the Galaxy S25 Ultra leading on zoom range and the iPhone 16 Pro Max leading on video consistency. The Xiaomi 15 Pro and Realme GT 6 both offer excellent camera performance for hundreds of dollars less.
Does more megapixels mean a better camera?
No. Megapixel count only measures resolution, not overall photo quality. Sensor size, lens aperture, optical image stabilization, and image processing software all affect real-world photo quality far more than the megapixel number printed on a spec sheet.
Which budget phone has the best camera in 2025?
The Infinix GT 20 Pro offers the strongest camera performance under $300 in 2025, while the Tecno Phantom X2 Pro stands out specifically for offering a periscope zoom lens, a feature normally reserved for phones costing far more.
Is optical zoom better than digital zoom on a phone camera?
Yes. Optical zoom uses a separate physical lens to magnify the image without losing detail, while digital zoom simply crops and enlarges the existing image, which reduces sharpness. A phone's optical zoom range is a much better indicator of real zoom quality than its total advertised zoom number.
Which phone is best for video recording in 2025?
The Apple iPhone 16 Pro Max leads this ranking for video, thanks to consistently smooth stabilization and closely matched color and exposure across its main, ultra-wide, and telephoto lenses, even in more demanding lighting conditions.
Why do some phones take better low-light photos than others with similar specs?
Low-light performance depends on sensor size, lens aperture, and computational photography software working together, not any single spec alone. Two phones with similar megapixel counts can produce very different low-light results depending on how well their processing combines multiple frames to reduce noise.
What is optical image stabilization and why does it matter?
Optical image stabilization, or OIS, physically shifts the camera lens or sensor to counteract small hand movements while shooting, which reduces blur in both photos and video. It is especially useful in low light or while recording video on the move.
Is a periscope zoom lens worth it on a mid-range phone?
If you regularly photograph distant subjects such as wildlife, sports, or travel landmarks, a periscope zoom lens like the one on the Tecno Phantom X2 Pro offers a genuine advantage over standard digital zoom, even on a phone that costs far less than a flagship.
Do camera phone rankings change between auto mode and pro mode?
Yes, sometimes significantly. A phone's manual or pro camera mode can produce noticeably different results than its default auto mode, since auto mode applies more automatic processing. Rankings based only on auto mode better reflect how most people actually use their camera day to day.
Which phone camera is best for selfies in 2025?
The Apple iPhone 16 Pro Max and Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra both produce sharp, natural-looking selfies with accurate skin tones, generally outperforming mid-range and budget phones in detail and dynamic range under mixed lighting.

Final Verdict

If you want the single most versatile camera phone of 2025 and budget is not the deciding factor, the Samsung Galaxy S25 Ultra remains the top overall pick thanks to its unmatched zoom range and strong low-light performance. If video quality matters more to you than zoom, the Apple iPhone 16 Pro Max is the safer choice. For buyers who want excellent photo quality without flagship pricing, the Xiaomi 15 Pro and Realme GT 6 both deliver outstanding value, while the Infinix GT 20 Pro and Tecno Phantom X2 Pro prove that a genuinely good camera phone no longer requires a four-figure budget.
